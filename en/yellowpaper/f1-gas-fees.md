# F.1 Gas & Fee Market

> **Design status**: proposed design. The fee model is a design proposal; concrete rates await testnet benchmarks ([Appendix II](appendix-parameters.md)). This section covers the protocol-level fee mechanism and does not touch token supply/allocation.

## F.1.1 Gas Metering

The resources consumed by each transaction are measured in **gas**, covering three cost categories:

$$\mathsf{gas}(\mathsf{tx}) = g_{\text{compute}} + g_{\text{state}} + g_{\text{data}}$$

* $g_{\text{compute}}$: the instruction cost of execution / validator / policy sandbox ([C.3](c3-policy-paymaster.md));
* $g_{\text{state}}$: the cost of state reads and writes (new accounts / storage cost more, suppressing state bloat, [B.3.5](b3-state.md));
* $g_{\text{data}}$: transaction byte and bandwidth cost.

The sandbox meters per instruction and aborts on budget overrun (OOG, [C.3.1](c3-policy-paymaster.md)) — gas is the pricing gate against DoS.

## F.1.2 A Payment-Grade Fee Model

General-purpose chains mostly use **auction-style** fees (EIP-1559 base fee floating with congestion + tip bidding). For payments this is fatal: a merchant cannot accept "a fee that jumps between 0.1 cent and 5 dollars" — it wrecks pricing, reconciliation, and the entire business model (whitepaper [3.3](../part3-architecture/3-3-consensus-finality.md)).

AXON's core trade-off: **low and predictable > merely lower**. The proposed design:

* **Steady-state fixed fee**: under normal load, payment/settlement-type transactions adopt a **fixed, predictable fee** — `fee = gas × price_fixed`, where $price_{\text{fixed}}$ is set by governance and adjusted smoothly, rather than auctioned per block. Fees should be as stable as infrastructure pricing, not swing like an auction price driven by sentiment.
* **Congestion pricing as a protection valve**: only when load persistently approaches the capacity ceiling does an EIP-1559-like congestion surcharge activate to suppress abuse; it does not trigger under normal payment traffic. This preserves predictability while retaining overload protection.

$$\text{fee}(\mathsf{tx}) = \mathsf{gas}(\mathsf{tx}) \times \big(price_{\text{fixed}} + \mathbb{1}[\text{congested}] \cdot price_{\text{surge}}(U_{\text{block}})\big)$$

Design Target: payment transaction fees land in the **sub-cent range** ($< \$0.001$, whitepaper performance Design Target), making pay-per-call M2M micropayments ([C.2.7](c2-session-keys.md)) economically viable.

## F.1.3 Fee Destination

Protocol fee revenue settles at the chain layer, forming AXON's real protocol revenue (whitepaper [3.1](../part3-architecture/3-1-why-own-l1.md)'s "value settles at the chain layer") — network sustainability comes from revenue generated by real business volume (TPV), not from inflationary subsidy. Mechanically, part of the protocol fees may be injected into the copy-trading reserve pool ([E.4.3](e4-reserve-risk.md)) at a governance-set ratio $\phi_{ct}$, as one of the sources feeding its risk-control buffer; the specific distribution ratio belongs to token economics, see below.

> How fee revenue is distributed among validators and the protocol treasury, and how it ties into token value capture, belongs to the **token economics** domain, which this yellowpaper does **not** expand on; see the "deflationary flywheel" in whitepaper [Part VII · Tokenomics](../part7-tokenomics/README.md). This section only specifies the **metering and pricing mechanism** of fees.

## F.1.4 Paymaster Economics

The economic safety of a Paymaster ([C.3.2](c3-policy-paymaster.md)) sponsoring gas: it locks a `max_fee` deposit before sponsoring, settles at the actually-paid amount after execution, refunds the balance, and can set per-user/per-period quotas to resist gas draining. The sponsor's business model (an app subsidizing user acquisition, a merchant bearing acquiring costs, etc.) is theirs to define; the protocol only guarantees that **the sponsor always remains solvent and cannot be arbitrarily freeloaded**.

## F.1.5 Synergy with Settlement

Because stablecoin settlement is a chain-layer primitive ([D.1](d1-settlement.md)), payment transactions have a short gas path and a predictable state-access pattern, so $g_{\text{state}}$/$g_{\text{compute}}$ vary little — this is precisely the technical premise that lets "fixed predictable fees" work. Combined with the Paymaster freeing users from holding a gas token, the payment experience can be made "as smooth as Web2" while retaining on-chain determinism.
